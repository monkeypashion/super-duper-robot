Germany 1890–1945: Democracy and Dictatorship

Kaiserreich & Prelude to Democracy

Under Kaiser Wilhelm, Germany was an authoritarian empire. While there was a parliamentary government, real power lay with the Kaiser. The state was shaped by Prussian militarism and driven by rapid industrialisation. Social tensions grew, leading to attempts at social reform and rising support for socialism. Ambitious Navy Laws reflected Germany's desire to rival Britain as a naval power.

First World War & Aftermath

The First World War left Germany in ruins. The impact of the war included war weariness, severe economic problems, and a sense of defeat which led to the end of the monarchy. The harsh reparations imposed by the Treaty of Versailles and the occupation of the Ruhr only worsened the crisis. The resulting hyperinflation destroyed public trust in the economy.

Weimar Republic: Unrest and Recovery

The new Weimar Republic was born in crisis. Early years saw violent political unrest, with threats from the left like the Spartacists and from the right with the Kapp Putsch. Even Hitler attempted a coup in the Munich Putsch. However, from 1924–1929, the Republic stabilised under Stresemann.

Key measures included a new currency, economic plans like the Dawes Plan and later the Young Plan. Internationally, Germany signed agreements that improved its status, such as the Locarno Pact and joined the League of Nations. Culturally, this was a time of experimentation and liberalism, known as Weimar culture.

Germany and the Depression

The Great Depression had devastating effects on Germany. Support grew rapidly for extremist parties between 1928 and 1932, with the Nazis using the SA to project strength and fear. Hitler's appeal helped fuel their rise.

As the Weimar system failed to deliver solutions, key figures like Papen and Hindenburg enabled Hitler’s rise, culminating in his appointment as Chancellor in 1933.

Establishment of Hitler’s Dictatorship

After Hitler’s appointment as Chancellor in 1933, the Nazis moved quickly to secure power. The Reichstag Fire allowed Hitler to suppress opposition and pass the Enabling Act, giving him legislative power. Political opposition was removed and trade unions were abolished.

In 1934, Hitler targeted internal threats with the Night of the Long Knives, eliminating the SA leadership and rivals. When President Hindenburg died, Hitler declared himself Führer of Germany, completing the creation of his dictatorship.

The Experiences of Germans under the Nazis

The Nazi regime introduced wide-ranging economic changes. Programmes like public works and rearmament reduced unemployment. The regime also promoted self-sufficiency. During the war, the economy shifted to a war footing, with severe consequences for civilians including bombing, rationing, labour shortages and the arrival of refugees.

Social policy focused on enforcing traditional roles. Women were encouraged to focus on the family and childbearing. Women were removed from many jobs and given awards for having many children. Youth groups became compulsory. Education was changed to promote Nazi ideology and loyalty to Hitler.

Religion was also controlled. The Nazis tried to coordinate Protestant churches under the Reich Church and suppressed dissent. Catholic churches retained some independence but were monitored closely.

Racial policies were central to Nazi ideology. The Nazis promoted Aryan ideas and enacted harsh racial policies. These escalated into the Final Solution during the war, resulting in the mass murder of six million Jews.

The Nazis used extensive control mechanisms. Goebbels directed propaganda, while censorship restricted free speech. Nazi culture promoted loyalty and unity. The police state was enforced by Himmler, the SS and the Gestapo.

Despite fear and repression, some groups resisted the regime. These included student-led initiatives like the White Rose, youth countercultures like the Swing Youth and Edelweiss Pirates, and military resistance such as the July 1944 bomb plot.

Conflict and Tension between East and West 1945–1972

Origins of the Cold War

After the Second World War, tensions grew between the USA and the USSR, shaped by differing ideologies and ambitions. The Yalta and Potsdam Conferences highlighted these divisions. Germany was divided, and Stalin, Roosevelt, Churchill, Attlee, and Truman all played major roles. The dropping of the atom bomb increased Soviet fears.

As Stalin expanded Soviet control over Eastern Europe, Churchill warned of an Iron Curtain. In response, the USA announced the Truman Doctrine and launched the Marshall Plan, which Stalin saw as a threat. He countered with Cominform and Comecon. Even Yugoslavia pursued a more independent path. The Berlin Blockade and Airlift became a major flashpoint.

Development of the Cold War

Events in Asia influenced superpower relations. The USSR supported Mao Zedong in China, and conflict followed in Korea and Vietnam. North Korea invaded the South in 1950, leading to UN military involvement, while the Vietcong resisted Western-backed control in Vietnam.

Military rivalry escalated through the arms race. The USA formed NATO, and the USSR responded with the Warsaw Pact. The space race saw milestones like Sputnik, ICBMs, Polaris, Gagarin, and the Apollo programme.

The ‘Thaw’ referred to a temporary easing of tensions. In Hungary, Nagy led a reform movement. Soviet suppression had lasting consequences. Later, the U2 Crisis sabotaged the Paris Peace Summit and stalled diplomatic progress.

Transformation of the Cold War

In 1961, the Soviets built the Berlin Wall. President Kennedy responded with public shows of resolve.

Cuba became a hotspot after Castro's revolution. The failed Bay of Pigs invasion embarrassed the USA. Tensions peaked during the missile crisis, involving Khrushchev and Kennedy. The crisis revealed the danger of escalation and led to increased communication.

In 1968, Dubček launched the Prague Spring. The USSR responded harshly, reaffirming control through the Brezhnev Doctrine, straining East-West relations.

Despite ongoing issues like human rights abuses, the late 1960s saw efforts at détente. SALT 1 was signed, and leaders like Brezhnev and Nixon promoted dialogue.

Britain: Migration, Empires and the People c790–present

Conquest and Identity

Britain’s story begins with Viking invasions and the emergence of the Danelaw. Figures like Alfred of Wessex and King Cnut helped forge early political unity. The relationship between England and France developed through the Angevin Empire, involving rulers like Henry II and King John.

Conflicts such as the Hundred Years’ War laid the groundwork for the development of an emerging English identity distinct from continental Europe.

Looking Westward: Empire and Migration

The rise of British influence in the Caribbean saw piracy and plunder turn into structured colonisation. The slave trade brought wealth and suffering, with figures like John Hawkins playing a key role. Settlements in Barbados and the West Indies fuelled Britain’s growing economic power.

Colonisation in North America followed, driven by figures like Raleigh and culminating in Jamestown. Relations with indigenous peoples, the rise of commodities, and migration of indentured servants all played a part. The loss of the American colonies was a turning point.

Migration within and to Britain included the Huguenots, the Highland clearances, and Ulster plantations, reshaping communities across Britain and Ireland.

Empire: East, South, and Africa

In India, the East India Company became a powerful force. Figures like Robert Clive and Warren Hastings expanded control, but the Indian Rebellion exposed tensions. Empire reshaped both India and Britain.

British expansion in Africa saw involvement in trade and missionary work, with significant activity in Egypt and South Africa. The Scramble for Africa was epitomised by Cecil Rhodes and the Boer War, often promoted via imperial propaganda.

Migration patterns broadened: Irish migration, Jewish migration, and transportation changed Britain's population. Movements included Asians to Africa and rural-urban shifts within Britain.

The 20th Century and Beyond

Two world wars hastened the end of Empire. The Suez Crisis symbolised decline. Nationalist leaders like Gandhi, Nkrumah and Kenyatta reshaped Africa and Asia.

Post-colonial Britain saw immigration from the Windrush generation. Activists like Claudia Jones helped define new British identities. Migration from Asia and Africa, the Commonwealth, and events like the Falklands War show empire’s continued relevance.

Britain’s relationship with Europe was shaped by war and trade. After the Second World War, economic and social links grew, culminating in membership of the EU. Migration patterns included European and non-European movement.

Elizabethan England c1568–1603

Elizabeth’s Court and Succession

Elizabeth I was a skilled ruler whose character and background shaped her rule. Her court centred on key figures and was based around patronage. Key ministers helped govern but she maintained final authority.

As a female monarch, Elizabeth faced pressure to marry and produce an heir. Tensions with Parliament persisted throughout her reign. By the end of her rule, her authority had waned slightly, shown in events like Essex’s Rebellion.

Life in Elizabethan Times

Elizabeth’s reign is often seen as a ‘Golden Age’, with improvements in living standards, and the rise of the gentry. Cultural achievement peaked in theatre, though not all supported it.

Poverty was growing, driven by economic changes. The government introduced laws to address the problem of the poor.

English sailors played a key role in expanding trade and challenging Spanish power. Drake’s circumnavigation demonstrated English naval prowess. Raleigh promoted overseas ventures.

Religious Division and Foreign Threats

Religion remained divisive. Elizabeth’s settlement faced opposition from Catholics and radical Puritans. Threats included the Northern Rebellion, plots backed by foreign powers, and her excommunication. In response, the state persecuted missionaries and punished conspirators.

Mary, Queen of Scots was a constant threat. Parliament urged Elizabeth to act. After plots involving her were uncovered, her execution had wide consequences.

Tensions with Spain escalated over trade, piracy, and Catholic plots. War broke out, culminating in the Spanish Armada. The conflict boosted Elizabeth’s prestige and demonstrated England’s growing naval power.
