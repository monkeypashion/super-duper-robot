Living with the Physical Environment

Section A – The Challenge of Natural Hazards

Natural Hazards

Natural hazards are extreme natural events that pose significant risk to people and property. The main types are tectonic, weather, and climate-related hazards. Risk is influenced by factors such as population density, level of preparedness, and economic development.

Tectonic Hazards

Physical Processes: Plate tectonics theory explains how earthquakes and volcanoes occur at different types of plate margins. These movements release energy or magma, causing hazards.

Effects & Responses: The primary effects and secondary effects of tectonic hazards vary between contrasting levels of wealth. Immediate responses may include rescue efforts, while long-term responses involve rebuilding and planning. Case studies show how wealth impacts the response.

Management: Despite the risk, people continue living in hazardous zones due to factors like family ties or economic benefits. Risk reduction involves monitoring, prediction, protection and planning.

Example: Chile & Nepal

Weather Hazards

Global Circulation: Atmospheric circulation helps explain global climate patterns.

Tropical Storms: These storms form in specific conditions and include cyclones and hurricanes. Their structure, development, and impact are shaped by temperature and wind conditions. Climate change could affect their frequency and strength.

Impacts & Management: Tropical storms can devastate communities and ecosystems. Effects vary widely and are shown through named examples. Risk is reduced through prediction, protection and preparation.

Example: Typhoon Haiyan

UK Weather Hazards: The UK experiences multiple weather hazards. Extreme events are increasingly common and affect infrastructure, economy and health. Case studies show causes, impacts and management of recent extreme events.

Example: Somerset Levels

Climate Change

Causes & Effects: Climate change is driven by natural factors and human activities. Evidence includes ice cores and temperature records. Effects are wide-ranging for people and ecosystems.

Managing Climate Change: Mitigation strategies include renewable energy, carbon capture, tree planting, and international agreements. Adaptation includes improving water supply, building flood defences and altering farming practices.

Section B – The Living World

Ecosystems

Ecosystems are communities where biotic and abiotic components interact. Small scale UK examples show how components like producers, consumers and decomposers form food chains and nutrient cycles. Changes to one part can affect the whole system. Also studied are the global distribution and features of large-scale ecosystems (biomes).

Example: Epping Forest

Tropical Rainforests

Characteristics: Rainforests have distinct features shaped by interdependence of climate, soil, vegetation, and fauna. High biodiversity and plant adaptation are key traits.

Deforestation: Driven by logging, farming, mining, settlement and energy demands. Impacts include environmental damage and economic trade-offs. Case studies show changing rates and impacts of deforestation.

Management: Sustainable management involves selective logging, replanting, education, ecotourism, and debt-for-nature agreements to conserve forest ecosystems.

Example: Amazon

Case Study: Amazon

Hot Deserts

Characteristics: Hot deserts also have distinct climates and ecosystems. Plants and animals adapt to survive in dry, hot conditions. Biodiversity is lower than in rainforests but equally interconnected.

Opportunities & Challenges: Development includes mining, tourism and farming. Challenges include extreme environments and accessibility. Case studies show how people adapt and develop in desert regions.

Desertification: Areas around deserts face the threat of desertification, caused by overgrazing, deforestation and climate change. Management includes water conservation, tree planting and better land use.

Example: Western Desert

Case Study: Sonoran / Western Desert

Section C – UK Physical Landscapes

UK Physical Landscapes

Diverse Landscapes: The UK has a range of diverse landscapes. Overview of major upland, lowland and river systems.

Coastal Landscapes in the UK

Processes: The coast is shaped by physical processes such as weathering, mass movement, erosion, transportation and deposition.

Landforms: Coastal landforms are shaped by erosional and depositional processes. Case studies show examples around the UK.

Management: Different strategies such as hard engineering and soft engineering aim to protect the coast. Managed retreat is also considered.

Glacial Landscapes in the UK

Glacial Processes: Glacial landscapes are formed by erosion, transportation and deposition.

Landforms: Erosional features include corries, troughs and truncated spurs. Depositional features include moraine and drumlins.

Land Use Conflicts: Upland glaciated areas offer opportunities for farming, forestry, tourism and quarrying. Conflicts arise and are managed with sustainable strategies. Case studies show impacts and solutions.

Example: Lake District

Resource Management in the UK
